Salud

An AI-powered, community-centered cancer screening experience designed with and for underserved Latino communities in Los Angeles.

Overview

Project at a glance

Salud is a service design project developed in partnership with Cedars-Sinai, focused on reducing barriers to cancer screening for underserved Latino communities. The project combines AI-driven personalization with human-centered service touchpoints to make screening accessible, trusted, and actionable.

Context

Why cancer screening falls short for Latino communities

Latino adults in the US are significantly less likely to receive timely cancer screenings compared to their white counterparts — not because of indifference, but because the system was not designed with them in mind.

Language barriers, distrust of medical institutions, lack of insurance, and logistical friction — transportation, time off work, childcare — compound to create a system that feels inaccessible, even hostile, to many.

Insights

What we heard, what we learned

Through contextual interviews, community shadowing, and co-design sessions with patients and community health workers, four critical insight clusters emerged.

01
"I don't know what questions to ask."

Patients felt underprepared and overwhelmed in clinical settings, leading to missed follow-throughs.
02
"I trust my neighbor more than a flyer."

Community health workers (promotoras) are the most trusted channel for health information in the community.
03
"The paperwork is in English."

Administrative burden — forms, portals, letters — is a significant drop-off point for non-English speakers.
04
"I didn't know I needed it."

Low awareness of screening eligibility and urgency, particularly for asymptomatic conditions like cervical cancer.

"When the letter arrived, it was all in English. I didn't understand it, so I put it aside. I never went."

— Interview participant, East LA, age 58
The Problem

How might we make cancer screening feel safe, clear, and worth showing up for?

The core tension: Cedars-Sinai has the clinical capability to reach these communities, but the existing touchpoints — digital portals, mailed letters, phone trees — are built for a patient who is already engaged with the healthcare system.

The problem is not awareness alone. It is trust, language, logistics, and the feeling that "this system is not for me." Our challenge was to work within existing infrastructure while creating moments that feel human, culturally resonant, and actionable.

Solution

A three-layer service: AI, community, and care coordination

Salud is not a single app — it is a service system that operates across digital, human, and physical touchpoints. The design centers a community health worker (promotora) as the trusted intermediary, supported by an AI screening assistant and simplified care coordination tools.

service blueprint — three-layer system

Service blueprint — community, digital, and clinical touchpoints working in concert

The AI assistant surfaces eligibility, answers questions in Spanish, and generates a simple "next step" card for the patient and their promotora. The promotora receives a coordinated dashboard — not a clinical EMR — designed for someone who is not a medical professional but is the most trusted link in the chain.

The Outcome

What we learned by designing in community

The project was presented to Cedars-Sinai's Community Health team in April 2025. The service blueprint and promotora dashboard were received as immediately actionable within their existing outreach infrastructure.

The most important lesson: effective health design requires meeting people in their existing networks of trust, not building new ones. The promotora is not a workaround — she is the system.
