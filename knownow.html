The Problem

Emergency alerts were built for English speakers

During the 2025 LA wildfires, over 12,000 of 50,000 Asian residents in four evacuation zones needed language assistance — yet alerts arrived in English and Spanish only. For non-English speaking communities, a language barrier in an emergency isn't an inconvenience. It's a life-safety failure.

Research

Going to where the crisis actually was

We didn't start in a classroom. We visited an active Red Cross emergency shelter, recruited interviewees through social media and physical posters, and conducted text-based outreach to first responders, bilingual community members, and emergency workers across LA.

Field visit — Red Cross emergency shelter, Los Angeles, January 2025

Participant recruiting — Instagram story outreach and physical poster in the community

Insights

Three things research kept telling us

During the LA Fire, over 12,000 of 50,000 Asian residents in the four evacuation zones required language assistance, yet alerts were issued in English and Spanish only.

"Most of the alerts came through our phones eventually, but they were in English, and understanding them was hard for me... I ended up calling a friend from the cultural association to help translate."

— HUA LI

6,387 non-English speaking residents in the Eaton Fire struggled to verify accuracy of information due to language barriers and a lack of trusted sources.

"I didn't get any official information right away. I don't really watch TV much because I'm busy working, and when I do, it's usually in Spanish because my English is still hard for me sometimes."

— MARIA LOPEZ (synthetic user)

More than 30 million adults in the U.S. can't read or write, and over 26.6% of the California population are LEP, while most alerts during LA fire were purely in English text.

"Everything seemed designed for people who already had more resources or who spoke the language perfectly."

— MARIA LOPEZ (synthetic user)
Who We Designed For

The most vulnerable user: a recent immigrant

From our user type matrix, we focused on the quadrant with the highest risk — limited English proficiency combined with no disaster readiness training. Our primary user is Li Xiao: 42, Cantonese-speaking, arrived from Guangzhou 4 months ago on a family reunification visa, living in a small apartment in LA's Cantonese community.

Concept

One storyboard scene captures the whole problem

Li Xiao gets an English-only wildfire alert at work. His wife texts him in panic — she can see smoke from their apartment. He can't tell if they need to evacuate. He opens KnowNow, which translates the alert into Cantonese, shows fire zones visually, and sends his wife a simple message: "Start getting ready, pack important items."

Prototype Process

Paper first, then iterate

We built and tested a paper prototype with 4 participants — product design students and a faculty member. Three rounds of key findings shaped our revisions: unclear delete interactions on the packing list, an unnoticeable alert icon, and a confusing shelter filter menu.

Paper prototype — language selection, alerts, shelter map, packing list

Revision 01
Packing list micro-interactions
Updated to left-swipe = delete, right-swipe = complete, tap = edit. Added disaster-type filter tabs.
Revision 02
Alert navigation
Moved alert button to center of bottom nav and scaled up the active disaster display.
Revision 03
Shelter filter
Replaced confusing filter menu with a Sort By dropdown and set current location as default.
